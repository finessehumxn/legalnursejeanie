Landmark Cases

The cases our field is built on.

CASE FILE · TENNESSEE, 2017

The Vaught medication error

A fatal drug swap, vecuronium administered instead of Versed, ended in a nurse's criminal conviction and changed how the entire profession views documentation. The chart lesson: override logs and scanning records tell the story long before testimony does.

CASE FILE · NEW YORK, 1984

Libby Zion

An eighteen year old's death under exhausted, unsupervised residents reshaped physician duty hours nationwide. The chart lesson: orders, timing, and who was actually present at each decision are all in the record.

CASE FILE · SOUTH CAROLINA, 2000

Lewis Blackman

A healthy teenager's post-surgical deterioration went unrecognized until it was too late, the failure to rescue that now carries his name in state law. The chart lesson: vital sign trends and escalation gaps are visible on paper hours before a crisis.

CASE FILE · MARYLAND, 2001

Josie King

A toddler's death from dehydration and communication breakdown at a world-renowned hospital launched the modern patient safety movement. The chart lesson: the record shows when warnings went undocumented and unheard.

These are public landmark matters studied across our field, not Obsidian engagements. Our insights library examines cases like these in depth, and what their records still teach.

Our Lead Nurse

Jeanie Vatelia, BSN, RN · Lead Nurse Consultant

Obsidian's consultants are practicing registered nurses. Jeanie Vatelia brings decades of clinical experience across pain management and orthopedic care, charting systems, and the details that decide cases, and puts that clinical eye to work for legal teams.

Licensed in California and Arizona, and holding APC registration in New Zealand, she approaches every engagement with the discipline of patient care: precise, documented, and honest about what the record does and does not show. She is also a passionate advocate for Black women in malpractice and injury cases, where symptoms are too often dismissed and pain under-documented, and she is known for catching what everyday practice misses: the small deviations in a chart that become the center of a case.
